GIS in Motion

This company is made up of 4 diverse individuals that cover a broad range of GIS professions, after all obtaining an undergraduate degree in Geoinformatics at the university of Pretoria, and soon forming a Geospatial Company afterwards. GIS in Motion team aims to formulate solutions based on the effective use of geographical information, and spatial and other data. The solutions maximise value for our customers by applying mathematical, and machine learning sciences to the unique spatial information.

Problem

The problem identified by the University of Pretoria: The students (and staff) are struggling to navigate through the campus efficiently and effectively, in order to arrive at class on time. The students have a limited time of 10 minutes between each class to get to their next lecture. Many students have reported that the current maps provided are confusing and take too long to navigate with.

Data

Collection:

The data collection process entails gathering the raw data necessary for this project. The main datasets we will be collecting are as follows, during peak and off peak times:

  • Foot Traffic: The number of people passing through the point on foot
  • Bicycles intersecting walkways: The number of people passing through the point on a bicycle or other mode of transportation
  • Surveys: Qualitative data, through the use of a multiple choice questionnaire
  • Roads/Paths: in order to accurately digitise
  • Buildings: in order to accurately digitise

Struggles:

We had a limited time period to collect the different data between each point, also, it was slightly embarrassing standing and watching people walk past us

32 points were selected through out the Hatfield Campus

Teamwork was essential!

Organisation:

Data Organization involves cleaning the data and putting it into a suitable format. The data collected was then organised into spreadsheets, and shapefiles and saved in a specified database.

Visualisation:

Data visualisation is very essential because this is where the clarification and communication of data to a broad audience takes place. We made use of bar charts, pie charts and heat maps to better communicate our findings which are related to modes of transportation, human traffic, entrances used, relaxation areas and finally, the congested areas in the University of Pretoria, Hatfield campus.
